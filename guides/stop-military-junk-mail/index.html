How to Stop Junk Mail from the Military

Military recruitment letters, USAA financial offers, VA loan pitches, and "Tricare-eligible" mailers — there are specific opt-outs for each. Here's the complete playbook.

"Military junk mail" isn't one thing — it's several distinct categories of mail, each with a different source and a different opt-out. Before you can stop it, you need to know what you're actually dealing with.

Stopping Military Recruitment Mail

If someone in your household is between 16–25, the Department of Defense's Joint Advertising Market Research & Studies (JAMRS) program maintains a database of recruiting prospects — and all four military branches draw from it. Getting off this list is the most effective single action for stopping recruitment mail.

Free · Covers All Military Branches · Official Opt-Out

JAMRS Opt-Out (DoD Recruiting Database)

The JAMRS database is shared by the Army, Navy, Air Force, Marines, and Coast Guard. Opting out removes your name from all of them simultaneously. You must mail a written request — there is no online form.

JAMRS Opt-Out Instructions →

Your written opt-out request should be mailed to:

Defense Manpower Data Center
ATTN: Recruiting Opt-Out
4800 Mark Center Drive
Suite 04J25-01
Alexandria, VA 22350-4000

Include: full name, date of birth, current address, and a clear statement that you do not consent to use of your information for military recruiting purposes. Responses take 60–90 days to propagate to all branches.

Opting Out of Individual Branch Recruiting

If mail from a specific branch continues after 90 days, contact that branch's recruiting command directly:

  • Army: goarmy.com → Contact Us → Opt Out of Mailings
  • Navy: navy.com/contact or call 1-800-USA-NAVY
  • Marines: marines.com → Contact → Request to Be Removed
  • Air Force: airforce.com → Contact Recruiter → Opt-Out Request
  • Coast Guard: gocoastguard.com → Contact

Stopping VA Loan Junk Mail

VA loan offers are not sent by the VA itself — they come from private lenders who buy lists of veteran-eligible borrowers from data brokers and credit bureaus. The VA does not sell your information, but once your veteran status is in data broker databases, it's available to anyone willing to pay for it.

Stopping USAA, Navy Federal, and Military Bank Offers

These institutions send marketing mail to members and non-members who appear on military-affiliated lists. The approach depends on your relationship with the company.

If you're a member

Log into your account, go to communication preferences, and disable all marketing mail. For USAA specifically: Profile → Contact Preferences → Mail → Marketing Communications → Off.

If you're not a member

Call or write to each company and request removal from their marketing list. You have no account relationship, so there's no portal — you'll need to contact them directly. USAA: 1-800-531-8722. Navy Federal: 1-888-842-6328. PenFed: 1-800-247-5626.

Stopping Military Charity and Veterans Organization Mail

Veterans organizations and military-adjacent charities — DAV, Wounded Warrior Project, American Legion, and dozens of smaller PACs — source their donor lists from data brokers and purchased mailing lists. DMAchoice registration covers many of them under the "non-profit" category.

For persistent senders: contact them directly and request removal, or use our Letter Generator to produce a formatted opt-out demand. Charities are legally required to honor opt-out requests for their own lists.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to opt out of military recruiting mail?

Yes. Under the Privacy Act and DoD regulations, individuals have the right to request removal from the JAMRS recruiting database. The military cannot compel you to receive recruiting communications.

I'm a veteran — why do I still get recruiting mail?

Recruiting databases don't always cross-reference against service records. If your address has changed since you enlisted, or if you're in a household with someone in the target age range, your address may appear on lists from multiple sources. The JAMRS opt-out and data broker removal together address this.

Can the USPS stop military mail from being delivered?

No. The USPS delivers all mail sent to your address — it cannot selectively block senders. Opt-outs must happen at the source (the organization sending the mail) or upstream (the data brokers providing the list).
